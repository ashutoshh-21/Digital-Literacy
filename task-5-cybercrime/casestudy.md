# Case Study: UPI/Online Payment Fraud
**Cybercrime Type:** UPI (Unified Payments Interface) / Online Payment Fraud  
**Scenario Type:** Realistic Fictional Scenario based on widely reported fraud patterns in India

---

## The Incident

Riya Sharma, a 20-year-old engineering student in Bhopal, listed her old laptop for sale on OLX for ₹18,000. Within hours, she received a call from someone claiming to be an Army officer posted in Pune who was "urgently" interested in buying it.

The caller seemed polite and credible. He said he would pay via Google Pay and asked Riya to "accept" ₹1 on her UPI app first to verify her account. He then sent her a **payment request** — not a transfer — framed as a "test transaction." Unfamiliar with the difference, Riya entered her UPI PIN to approve it, unknowingly authorising a ₹17,500 debit from her account.

The scammer disconnected immediately. The money was gone within seconds.

---

## How It Happens — Step by Step

1. Fraudster finds a seller listing on OLX/Facebook Marketplace.
2. Builds trust using a fake identity (often a military or government persona).
3. Sends a **UPI collect/payment request** disguised as a "payment."
4. Pressures the victim to enter their PIN to "receive" money.
5. Money is debited; scammer vanishes.

---

## Who Is Targeted

- First-time online sellers (students, homemakers)
- People unfamiliar with how UPI collect requests work
- Anyone in urgent need of money

---

## Consequences

- Immediate financial loss (often ₹5,000–₹50,000)
- Emotional distress and loss of trust in digital payments
- Difficult to recover funds; banks rarely reverse UPI transactions
- Psychological impact: shame, anxiety, reluctance to use digital platforms

---

## Key Lesson

> **You NEVER need to enter your UPI PIN to RECEIVE money. Entering your PIN always means you are PAYING.**
